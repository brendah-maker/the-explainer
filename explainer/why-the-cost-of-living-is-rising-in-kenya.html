Why the Cost of Living in Kenya Keeps Rising

Published March 2026

The rising cost of living has become one of the most pressing economic concerns for many households in Kenya. Over the past few years, the prices of essential goods such as food, fuel, electricity, and transport have steadily increased, placing significant pressure on families and businesses.

While economic growth continues in certain sectors, many citizens feel that their incomes are not keeping pace with the rising cost of everyday life. Economists point to a combination of global and domestic factors that are driving these price increases.

Key drivers of rising living costs in Kenya include: • Higher taxes and government levies • Rising global fuel prices • Depreciation of the Kenyan shilling • Global supply disruptions • Domestic economic challenges

Government Policy and Taxes

Government taxation policies play a significant role in determining the price of goods and services. In recent years, several taxes and levies have been introduced or increased in Kenya. These include higher taxes on fuel, cooking gas, and other essential items that form part of everyday household consumption.

In addition to taxes on goods, workers have also seen increased mandatory deductions from their salaries. Policies such as the Housing Levy and contributions to the Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF) reduce the disposable income workers take home each month.

Fuel and Transport Costs

Fuel prices are another major driver of the rising cost of living in Kenya. Because the country imports nearly all its petroleum products, local fuel prices are heavily influenced by global oil markets.

When international oil prices rise, transport costs increase across the economy. Since most goods are transported by road, higher fuel costs make it more expensive to move food, construction materials, and manufactured goods across the country.

Public transportation fares also tend to rise when fuel becomes more expensive, increasing commuting costs for workers and students.

Depreciation of the Kenya Shilling

Another important factor influencing the cost of living is the depreciation of the Kenyan shilling against major global currencies such as the US dollar.

When the shilling weakens, imported goods become more expensive. Kenya imports essential commodities including fuel, fertilizers, wheat, cooking oil, machinery, and industrial inputs. Businesses must therefore spend more Kenyan shillings to purchase these imports.

These higher costs are often passed on to consumers through increased prices, contributing to inflation across the economy.

Global Economic Pressures

Global events have also influenced the cost of living in Kenya. Supply chain disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic slowed production and transportation worldwide, leading to shortages and higher prices for many commodities.

Geopolitical conflicts such as the Russia-Ukraine war have also affected global markets. These countries are major exporters of wheat and fertilizers. When supplies are disrupted, prices rise globally, affecting import-dependent countries like Kenya.

Domestic Economic Challenges

Kenya has also faced several domestic economic challenges. Persistent budget deficits have required the government to rely heavily on borrowing to finance public spending. As debt obligations increase, fewer resources may be available for subsidies or economic support programs that help reduce living costs.

Structural issues such as inefficiencies in markets, governance challenges, and supply chain bottlenecks can also contribute to higher prices across the economy.

Impact on Kenyan Households

For many households, the combined effect of these factors has resulted in reduced purchasing power. Salaries often increase more slowly than prices, meaning families must spend a larger share of their income on essential goods and services.

Many households respond by cutting back on discretionary spending, seeking cheaper alternatives, or reducing non-essential purchases.

Looking Ahead

While the Kenyan economy continues to grow in sectors such as finance, technology, and services, policymakers face the challenge of ensuring that economic growth translates into improved living standards for ordinary citizens.

Addressing the rising cost of living will likely require a combination of fiscal discipline, improved economic management, investments in local production, and policies aimed at strengthening household purchasing power.
