PAYE Explained: How Income Tax Is Calculated in Kenya

Published March 2026

Pay As You Earn (PAYE) is the system used in Kenya to collect income tax from employees. Under this system, employers deduct income tax directly from an employee’s salary before it is paid and remit the tax to the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA).

The system ensures taxes are collected regularly throughout the year rather than requiring employees to make large annual payments. PAYE uses a progressive tax system, meaning higher portions of income are taxed at higher rates.

Step 1: Calculate Gross Taxable Income

The first step in calculating PAYE is determining the employee’s gross taxable income. This includes the basic salary as well as allowances, bonuses, commissions, overtime payments and other employment benefits.

Step 2: Deduct Allowable Deductions

After determining gross income, certain deductions are subtracted to determine taxable pay. The most common deduction is the National Social Security Fund (NSSF) contribution. Pension contributions may also reduce taxable income.

Step 3: Apply the Monthly Tax Bands

Kenya uses progressive tax bands introduced under the Finance Act 2023. Each portion of income is taxed at a different rate.

  • First KSh 24,000 – taxed at 10%
  • Next KSh 8,333 – taxed at 25%
  • Next KSh 467,667 – taxed at 30%
  • Next KSh 300,000 – taxed at 32.5%
  • Income above KSh 800,000 – taxed at 35%

Because the system is progressive, only the portion of income within each band is taxed at that specific rate.

Step 4: Subtract Personal Relief

After calculating the gross tax, employees receive a personal relief of KSh 2,400 per month. This relief directly reduces the tax payable.

Example PAYE Calculation

Employee salary: **KSh 50,000** First 24,000 at 10% = **KSh 2,400** Next 8,333 at 25% = **KSh 2,083** Remaining 17,667 at 30% = **KSh 5,300** Gross tax ≈ **KSh 9,783** After personal relief of **KSh 2,400** Final PAYE ≈ **KSh 7,383**

Other Statutory Deductions

Apart from PAYE, employees also contribute to other statutory deductions including the Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF) and the Housing Levy. These deductions reduce take-home salary but are not part of PAYE.

When PAYE Is Paid

Employers must remit PAYE to the Kenya Revenue Authority by the **9th day of the following month**. Late payment may result in penalties and interest.

Why PAYE Matters

PAYE is one of the largest sources of government revenue in Kenya and helps finance public services such as infrastructure, healthcare, education, and security.

Understanding how PAYE works helps employees verify their payslips and plan their finances more effectively.
