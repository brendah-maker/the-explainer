Why the Kenyan Shilling Weakens: Understanding Currency Depreciation

Published March 2026 • 7 min read

The value of the Kenyan shilling has experienced noticeable fluctuations over the past decade, often weakening against major international currencies such as the US dollar. Exchange rates are influenced by a wide range of economic forces, both domestic and global.

While short-term movements may be caused by temporary supply shocks or speculation, long-term trends usually reflect deeper structural conditions within the economy.

Common reasons the Kenyan shilling weakens: • Kenya imports more than it exports • Rising public debt and foreign repayments • Global interest rate changes • High demand for US dollars • Global commodity price increases

Trade Imbalance

Kenya imports significantly more goods and services than it exports, creating what economists call a trade deficit. The country relies heavily on imported products such as petroleum, machinery, vehicles, fertilizers, and electronics.

These imports must be paid for using foreign currencies, particularly the US dollar. Meanwhile, Kenya earns foreign currency primarily through exports such as tea, coffee, horticulture, tourism, and remittances from Kenyans abroad.

When import payments exceed export earnings, demand for dollars rises faster than supply. This imbalance puts pressure on the shilling, causing it to weaken over time.

Public Debt and Fiscal Deficits

Government borrowing also influences currency stability. Kenya has borrowed heavily in recent years to finance infrastructure projects and cover budget deficits. Much of this borrowing is denominated in foreign currencies.

Servicing external debt requires the government to regularly purchase foreign currency to repay interest and principal. This increases demand for dollars in the market, putting downward pressure on the shilling.

Global Economic Factors

Global financial conditions can also affect the value of emerging market currencies. When major economies raise interest rates, investors often move their money toward those markets because they offer higher returns.

As capital flows toward developed economies, countries like Kenya may experience capital outflows. Investors may sell local assets and convert their money into dollars, increasing demand for foreign currency.

Global commodity prices also play a role. For example, higher oil prices increase Kenya’s fuel import bill, which raises the demand for dollars.

Inflationary Pressures

A weaker currency often leads to higher domestic prices. Because Kenya imports many essential goods such as fuel, wheat, cooking oil, and industrial inputs, a weaker shilling makes these imports more expensive.

This process is known as imported inflation. As businesses face higher input costs, they may raise prices to maintain profit margins, contributing to rising inflation across the economy.

Market Dynamics and Policy Responses

The value of the Kenyan shilling is largely determined by supply and demand in international currency markets. However, the Central Bank of Kenya sometimes intervenes to stabilize the currency during periods of volatility.

Policy tools include adjusting interest rates, managing foreign exchange reserves, or directly participating in currency markets. These actions can help maintain stability and investor confidence.

Impact on the Economy

Currency depreciation has both positive and negative effects. A weaker shilling can make Kenyan exports more competitive in global markets, potentially boosting export earnings.

However, the negative effects are often felt more immediately. Higher import costs increase fuel prices, transport costs, and food prices, reducing purchasing power for households.

Conclusion

The movement of the Kenyan shilling reflects a complex interaction of domestic policies, global financial trends, and trade dynamics. Trade deficits, rising debt obligations, global interest rate changes, and market forces all influence the currency’s value.

Strengthening the shilling in the long term will likely require stronger export growth, improved fiscal discipline, increased foreign investment, and a more diversified economy.
