Why the Cost of Living in Kenya Keeps Rising

Published March 2026

The rising cost of living has become one of the most pressing economic concerns for many households in Kenya. Over the past few years, the prices of essential goods such as food, fuel, electricity, and transport have steadily increased, placing significant pressure on families and businesses. While economic growth continues in certain sectors, many citizens feel that their incomes are not keeping pace with the rising cost of everyday life.

Economists point to a combination of global and domestic factors that are driving these price increases. Government taxation policies, higher fuel costs, currency depreciation, global supply disruptions, and structural challenges within the economy all contribute to the situation. Understanding these factors helps explain why prices continue to rise and why many households are experiencing reduced purchasing power.

Government Policy and Taxes

Government taxation policies play a significant role in determining the price of goods and services. In recent years, several taxes and levies have been introduced or increased in Kenya. These include higher taxes on fuel, cooking gas, and other essential items that form part of everyday household consumption.

In addition to taxes on goods, workers have also seen increased mandatory deductions from their salaries. Policies such as the Housing Levy and the Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF) contributions require employees to contribute a portion of their income toward government programs. While these initiatives are intended to support housing development and healthcare access, they also reduce the disposable income that workers take home at the end of each month.

When disposable income falls while prices continue to rise, households often find it more difficult to meet their daily expenses. This dynamic has contributed to widespread concerns about affordability across many sectors of the economy.

Fuel and Transport Costs

Fuel prices are another major driver of the rising cost of living in Kenya. The country imports nearly all of its petroleum products, which means local prices are heavily influenced by global oil markets. When international oil prices rise, Kenyan consumers often feel the impact almost immediately through higher pump prices.

Fuel costs affect far more than just transportation. Because most goods in Kenya are transported by road, higher fuel prices increase the cost of moving products from farms, factories, and ports to markets and supermarkets. As a result, the cost of transporting food, construction materials, and manufactured goods rises, which is eventually passed on to consumers.

Public transportation fares are also closely tied to fuel prices. When fuel becomes more expensive, transport operators typically increase fares in order to maintain their operating margins. This increases commuting costs for workers and students, further raising the overall cost of living.

Depreciation of the Kenya Shilling

Another important factor influencing the cost of living is the depreciation of the Kenya shilling against major global currencies such as the US dollar. When the shilling weakens, it becomes more expensive for the country to import goods from abroad.

Kenya imports many essential commodities, including fuel, fertilizers, wheat, cooking oil, machinery, and industrial inputs. A weaker currency means businesses must spend more Kenyan shillings to purchase these imports. To cover the higher costs, businesses often raise the prices of the final products they sell to consumers.

This process contributes to inflation, which is the general increase in prices across the economy. As inflation rises, households can afford fewer goods and services with the same amount of income.

Global Economic Pressures

Global events have also played a role in shaping the cost of living in Kenya. Over the past few years, disruptions in international supply chains have affected the availability and pricing of many essential commodities. These disruptions were initially triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic, which slowed production and transportation across the world.

More recently, geopolitical conflicts such as the Russia-Ukraine war have further disrupted global markets. Both countries are major exporters of wheat, fertilizers, and other agricultural products. When supplies are disrupted, prices rise globally, and import-dependent countries like Kenya experience the ripple effects through higher food and farming costs.

Rising fertilizer prices, for example, increase the cost of farming. Farmers may respond by raising the prices of their produce to cover production expenses, which ultimately contributes to higher food prices for consumers.

Domestic Economic Challenges

In addition to global pressures, several domestic economic challenges also affect the cost of living. Kenya has faced persistent budget deficits, meaning government spending has often exceeded revenue collection. To bridge this gap, the country has relied heavily on borrowing, both locally and internationally.

Managing public debt has become an increasingly important issue in recent years. Debt repayments require significant government resources, which can limit the funds available for subsidies or public support programs that might otherwise help cushion citizens from rising costs.

Structural issues such as inefficiencies in markets, governance challenges, and corruption have also been frequently cited as factors that contribute to higher prices and economic strain. When resources are misallocated or inefficiencies persist in supply chains, the final cost of goods and services can increase.

Impact on Kenyan Households

For many Kenyan households, the combined effect of these factors is a noticeable decline in purchasing power. Salaries and wages often increase slowly compared with the speed at which prices rise. This means families must allocate a larger share of their income toward essential expenses such as food, housing, and transportation.

In response, households may adjust their spending habits by reducing discretionary purchases, seeking cheaper alternatives, or cutting back on non-essential items. Small businesses also face challenges as higher operating costs reduce profit margins and limit expansion.

Looking Ahead

While the Kenyan economy has shown signs of resilience and growth in sectors such as technology, finance, and services, the benefits of this growth have not always translated into improved living standards for all citizens. Policymakers continue to explore strategies to stabilize the economy, control inflation, and promote sustainable growth.

Addressing the cost of living challenge will likely require a combination of fiscal discipline, improved economic management, investments in local production, and policies that strengthen the purchasing power of households. Until these measures take full effect, the issue of rising living costs will remain a central topic in Kenya’s economic discussions.
